Virtual twins

A virtual twin, or digital twin, is a computational model of an individual patient that can be used to simulate and predict how that patient will respond to a given dose.

Definition

A virtual twin is built from a patient's own data (age, weight, genetics, laboratory values, disease markers and concomitant medicines) combined with a mechanistic description of how the body handles a drug and how the drug acts. Three properties distinguish a true twin from an ordinary model: it represents an individual patient, it integrates heterogeneous data, and it is updated iteratively as new data arrive, a process referred to as “twinning”.

The modelling techniques

Physiologically based pharmacokinetics (PBPK)

A whole-body model of absorption, distribution, metabolism and excretion.

02
Population PK/PD

Statistical models of how patients differ and of the dose–concentration–effect relationship.

03
Quantitative systems pharmacology (QSP)

Mechanistic models linking a drug to disease biology.

04
Combined approaches

Integrating the above with patient data.

Why virtual twins suit rare disease

In rare diseases, patients are few, geographically dispersed and often children, and conventional trials are difficult. A virtual twin allows questions to be explored in silico rather than on the patient: the effect of a higher dose, a possible drug–drug interaction, or the likely course of disease. Because a repurposed drug already has a known safety profile, its pharmacokinetics can be predicted for an individual patient without a new physical trial.

The current state, and where TWIN-EB fits

A recent review found that no existing rare-disease model yet meets the full definition of a true digital twin, chiefly because iterative, real-time updating is not implemented, so the field remains at an early stage. TWIN-EB is designed to address these gaps and follows the model-informed drug development and precision-dosing (MIDD/MIPD) credibility standards recognised by the FDA and EMA. A virtual twin supports a clinician's judgement rather than replacing it, and is only as reliable as the data and validation behind it.
