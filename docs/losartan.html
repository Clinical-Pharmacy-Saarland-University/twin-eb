The rationale

Losartan in RDEB

Losartan is a long-established blood-pressure medicine that also inhibits TGF-beta signalling, the pathway that drives fibrosis in RDEB. This makes it a candidate to slow the disease, but the right dose for children with RDEB is not yet established.

An anti-fibrotic mechanism

Losartan is an angiotensin II type 1 (AT1) receptor antagonist, licensed since 1995 for high blood pressure and approved for children from six years of age. Beyond blood pressure, it reduces signalling through the TGF-beta pathway, the same pathway that is hyperactivated in RDEB and drives scarring and fibrosis. Losartan has already been repurposed for the fibrosis of Marfan syndrome on this basis.

The evidence so far

Laboratory and animal studies

In RDEB mouse models, losartan reduced scarring and delayed forepaw deformity. In cultured human RDEB fibroblasts it lowered TGF-beta, type I collagen and thrombospondin-1.

The REFLECT trial

A Phase I/II study in 29 children with RDEB, aged 2–14, carried out between 2016 and 2021 and led by Prof. Dr. Dimitra Kiritsi. Losartan was well tolerated and was associated with clinical improvement.

The open question: the right dose

Despite this promising evidence, the optimal dose of losartan in children with RDEB is not established, and its pharmacokinetics in this population are poorly understood. RDEB changes how the body absorbs, distributes, metabolises and clears a drug: the gastrointestinal mucosa is altered, body composition differs, and many children are fed through a gastrostomy. As a result, standard adult or paediatric dosing may not translate, and a dose cannot simply be scaled by body weight. A regulatory (EMA) scientific advice recommended collecting pharmacokinetic data in children with RDEB because absorption may differ in this group.

Why this matters for dosing

Getting the dose right requires knowing how much drug reaches the circulation and how it behaves over time in these children. That is what TWIN-EB sets out to determine, by combining mechanistic modelling with a prospective pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic study.
