The disease

Epidermolysis bullosa and RDEB

Epidermolysis bullosa (EB) is a group of rare inherited disorders that make the skin fragile, so that minor friction causes blistering and wounds. Recessive dystrophic EB (RDEB) is one of its most severe forms.

What EB is

EB is caused by mutations affecting the structural proteins that anchor the epidermis to the dermis. It affects an estimated 500,000 people worldwide and includes several types (simplex, junctional and dystrophic). People with EB are sometimes called “butterfly children” because their skin is as fragile as a butterfly’s wing.

Recessive dystrophic EB (RDEB)

RDEB is caused by mutations in the COL7A1 gene, which reduce or abolish type VII collagen. Without functional collagen VII the anchoring fibrils that hold the epidermis to the dermis fail, and the skin separates below the basement membrane. Minor trauma then causes blistering, open wounds and, over time, scarring.

The burden of RDEB

Skin and wounds

Chronic wounds cover more than 30% of the body surface in around 60% of patients; pain and itch are common.

Progressive fibrosis

Repeated scarring leads to fusion of the fingers, known as pseudosyndactyly, joint contractures and narrowing of the oesophagus.

Systemic disease

A chronic proinflammatory state driven by hyperactivated TGF-beta signalling, with growth failure, anaemia, and feeding difficulties; a gastrostomy is needed in 8–58% of patients.

Current care

Care is largely supportive, centred on wound management, pain and nutrition, and prevention of infection. A few disease-modifying treatments have appeared recently (a topical birch-bark gel approved in the EU in 2022, and a topical gene therapy approved by the FDA in 2023), but there is still no established systemic, easily administered medicine that slows the disease itself.
